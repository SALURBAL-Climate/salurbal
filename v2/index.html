Salud Urbana en América Latina (SALURBAL), is a five-year project launched in April 2017. The Drexel University Dornsife School of Public Health and partners throughout Latin America and in the United States are working together to study how urban environments and urban policies impact the health of city residents throughout Latin America.

How to quantify the contributions of city-level Built Environment (BE) factors to differences in levels of health and health inequalities among and within cities?


The BE significantly affects the public’s health. For more than a decade there has been an increase in the collaborations among public health, social sciences and urban planning researchers to study how the BE may impact health and health inequities. Several features of Latin American cities (LAC) make them uniquely suited for the study of their urban environment. One approach to managing the complexity and volume of the data describing the urban settings and environmental characteristics of LAC is to develop BE typologies for sub-cities. These typologies are helpful because they help us understand how multiple dimensions of the BE come together and create profiles with these settings.

Sub-cities are derived from the administrative boundary of the respective city.

The methodology was implemented for 1.351 sub-cities in 10 Latin American countries.

Geolocation shown in the map is an approximation from real sub-city centroid. This modification is performed to avoid overlapping among points.

Analytical process for building the typlogies was developed in two domains: Urban Landscape and Street Design. Next slides present the process developed for the Urban Landscape domain and using 6 of its variables previously selected by domain experts.

Variables selected are: Area-Weigthed Mean Shape Index, Effective Mesh Size, Area-Weigthed Mean Nearest Neighbor, Patch Density, Number of Urban Patches, Area-Weighted Mean Patch Size.

Urban Landscape characterizes the distribution of the urban patches. A patch is an area of uninterrupted urban development. To determine the typologies in this domain we selected variables in three subdomains: fragmentation, shape and isolation. The fragmentation subdomain measures the division of the patches among the urban extent boundary and was quantified using the following variables: number of patches, patch density, area-weighted mean patch size and effective mesh size. The shape subdomain measures the average shape of the aggregated patches in the urban extent and was quantified using the area-weighted mean shape index variable. The isolation subdomain measures the average distance between urban patches and was quantified using the area-weighted mean nearest neighbor variable.

Area-Weighted Mean Shape Index is a ratio of the actual perimeter of a patch to the minimum perimeter possible for a maximally compact patch with the same size. The area-weighted mean shape index is the weighted average of the shape index for each patch within the geographic boundary. This index is weighted by the area of each patch.
