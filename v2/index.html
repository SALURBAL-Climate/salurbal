Salud Urbana en América Latina (SALURBAL), is a five-year project launched in April 2017. The Drexel University Dornsife School of Public Health and partners throughout Latin America and in the United States are working together to study how urban environments and urban policies impact the health of city residents throughout Latin America.

More specifically, we address in this sudy...


How to quantify the contributions of city-level Built Environment (BE) factors to differences in levels of health and health inequalities within and between cities?


The BE significantly affects the public’s health. For more than a decade there has been an increase in the collaborations among public health, social sciences and urban planning researchers to study how the BE may impact health and health inequities. Several features of Latin American cities (LAC) make them uniquely suited for the study of their urban environment. One approach to managing the complexity and volume of the data describing the urban settings and environmental characteristics of LAC is to develop BE profiles for cities and sub-cities. These profiles are helpful because they help to understand how multiple dimensions of the BE come together in like cities.

Sub-cities are derived from the administrative boundary of the respective city.

Analytical process for building the profiles was developed in two domains: Urban Landscape and Street Design.


Urban Landscape characterizes the distribution of the urban patches. A patch is an area of uninterrupted urban development. To determine the profile in this domain we selected variables in three subdomains: fragmentation, shape and isolation. The fragmentation subdomain measures the division of the patches among the urban extent boundary and was quantified using the following variables: number of patches, patch density, area-weighted mean patch size and effective mesh size. The shape subdomain measures the average shape of the aggregated patches in the urban extent and was quantified using the area-weighted mean shape index variable. The isolation subdomain measures the average distance between urban patches and was quantified using the area-weighted mean nearest neighbor variable.

Variables selected are: Area-Weigthed Mean Shape Index, Effective Mesh Size, Area-Weigthed Mean Nearest Neighbor, Patch Density, Number of Urban Patches, Area-Weighted Mean Patch Size.

The Number of Urban Patches variable measures the contiguous areas of urban development (urban patches hereafter).

💡 Argentina is the country with the highest variability ranging from 46,25 (quartile 25%) and 268,5 (quartile 75%) indicating a high presence of cities with small and large urban development size. Brasil is the country with the highest number of urban patches with a value upper than 3.000 located in Brasilia. Peru is the country with the lowest variability from the selected countries.

As in the previous chart, here is presented the distribution of the Area-Weighted Mean Shape Index (y-axis) by country (x-axis).


The Area-Weighted Mean Shape Index variable is a ratio of the actual perimeter of a patch to the minimum perimeter possible for a maximally compact patch with the same size. The Area-Weighted Mean Shape Index is the weighted average of the shape index for each patch within the geographic boundary. This index is weighted by the area of each patch.

💡 Guatemala, Chile, Brasil and Argentina present a high variability in this variable indicating the presence of cities with different shape ranging from compact cities (lower values) to complex cities (higher values).

Based on the variables previously mentioned, 6 Urban Landscape profiles are identified applying clustering algorithms. As showed in left chart, the profiles are identified by different color hues.


At this point you are able to come back to a previous slide keeping the color hue encoding for the profiles.

💡 Approximately 26% of the sub-cities were identified in the profile 5, being this the typology with the highest proportion of sub-cities in 50% of the countries. This pattern indicates a tendency of countries to design cities in similar way. The profile 6 has the lowest number of identified sub-cities. The 70% of non-indetified sub-cities were assigned to the other typologies. The profile 1, profile 2 and profile 4 are prominent in similar proportion in countries.

To identify the contribution of each variable to the identified profiles, a scatter plot matrix shows the 6 variables in the y and x-axis. As described in previous slide, color hue is used to represent the assigned profile. Sub-cities can be selected by a drag selection or by country.
